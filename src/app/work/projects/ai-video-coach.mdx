## The starting point

Meritt is a B2B sales recruitment platform where candidates create profiles, complete video introductions, and get matched with hiring companies. The video introduction was central to the product. It was the primary thing we sold to employers, a way to evaluate candidates beyond a CV.

To incentivise candidate completion we used a third-party supplier to create personality profiles, based on the Big 5 personality test. 

After launch completion rates averaged ~30%. Being central to the product, and after questions from prospects and customers as to the 'low' number and a perceived lack of value (and subsequent missed sales).

To understand the problem, I first checked the funnels in Posthog to confirm there weren't any clear dropoff points. Then I began having conversations with candidates. Their feedback consistently told me the same thing. Candidates found the video introduction and personality profile "nice" but didn't believe or see what it did for them personally. They completed it because the platform asked them to, not because they wanted to. For a feature that was core of the product, that was a concern.

There was a second pressure point. The tool worked, but it was expensive, tied us into a specific psychological framework that didn't align with where we wanted the product positioned, and the supplier was planning changes to their commercial model. We were paying for something that constrained us strategically and might not be available on the same terms for much longer.

## The opportunity

Two problems were sitting next to each other. Candidates weren't engaged with the video feature, and we were dependent on a costly external tool that didn't fit our direction. The question became whether we could solve both at once.

The video introduction already captured rich data. Candidates were speaking on camera for 90 to 120 seconds about their experience, motivation, and approach to sales. All of that signal was being used in one direction only: employers watched the videos. Candidates recorded them and moved on.

My hypothesis based on the candidate interviews was to increase the value provided to candidates, beyond just improving their applications.

This came about due to another feedback that was regularly received. The candidates didn't know whether their video was 'good' or 'the right thing'. 

If we could analyse the video and return genuinely useful coaching feedback to candidates, the video introduction would shift from being a task they completed for employers to something they actively wanted to do for themselves. That would drive completion rates up, improve the quality of what employers received, and give us a reason to retire the personality profiling tool entirely.

## The solution & validation

Before committing engineering time to a full build, I needed to understand two things: what kind of feedback would candidates actually find useful, and could we generate it reliably from video data?

After searching and investigating several options, we sourced an external supplier that provides emotional and tonal analysis on video and audio. This gave us a raw data layer covering vocal delivery, emotional expression, confidence signals, and communication patterns. The analysis was solid, but raw data alone wasn't useful to a candidate. Someone doesn't benefit from knowing their "vocal energy score is 0.73." They benefit from hearing "you tend to drop your energy at the end of answers, which can make you sound less convinced of what you're saying."

To bridge that gap, we took the output and ran it through a python microservice to extract the main emotions, then ran a generative AI feedback layer on the output. The critical step here was how we trained the tone and substance of the output.

Our CEO, who has deep expertise in sales hiring, worked alongside senior recruiters at our customers to define what good videos and hence good feedback looks like. We mapped the raw analysis data to the kind of observations these experts would actually make when reviewing a candidate's video. The AI wasn't generating generic advice. It was producing feedback grounded in what experienced sales hiring professionals would say.

With the analysis capability mocked up by engineering, I used Claude Code to rapidly build several versions of the results and feedback UI. This let us move fast on the presentation layer without pulling engineering off the core integration work.

I then put working prototypes in front of real candidates using their own videos (with their permission). We had open discussions about what information they found most useful, what surprised them, and what felt generic or unhelpful. We ran the same process with employer customers to make sure the feature aligned with what they valued in candidate profiles.

This feedback loop shaped everything from the specific metrics we surfaced to the language the AI used. Some data points that looked interesting in isolation turned out to be confusing or valueless for candidates. Others that seemed minor were the ones people latched onto and found actionable.

One example of this was feedback that was:
> "Detected 12 filler words across 90 seconds"

Which is useful but not particularly actionable. So it became:
> "when you're transitioning between topics, you fill the gap with 'um' and 'sort of.' Practise landing one point, pausing, then starting the next."

## What we shipped

The final feature analysed a candidate's video introduction and returned a personalised coaching page covering their communication style, delivery strengths, areas for improvement, and specific suggestions for their next interview. The feedback was conversational and actionable rather than clinical or score-based.

![Feedback given to candidates by the coach](/images/projects/ai-video-coach/ai-coach-feedback.png)
_Example of feedback given by the AI Coach_

It was built and shipped the full feature in a two-week sprint, covering the external API integration, the AI feedback microservice, the frontend results experience, and the updated candidate flow.

## The results

Within the first month of launch:

- **1,500+ candidates** used the coaching feature
- **Completion rates increased from 30% to 48%**, a meaningful shift for a feature that underpins the entire platform
- **92% positive feedback**, up from 70% on the previous experience
- The third-party personality profiling tool was retired, removing a significant cost and a strategic dependency

Beyond the numbers, the positioning of the video introduction changed across the entire product. What had been framed as "record a video for employers" became "get personalised coaching on your interview skills." Marketing, email sequences, landing pages, and onboarding flows all needed updating to reflect the new value proposition.

## What I learned

The rollout itself was smooth from an engineering perspective. Where I underestimated the effort was in the communications and branding work required to reposition the feature across every candidate touchpoint. What started as a feature improvement became a strategic product positioning shift, and the volume of emails, landing pages, and onboarding copy that needed rewriting added time I hadn't accounted for. For future launches of this scale, I'd map out the full surface area of downstream content changes earlier in planning.

The harder lesson was about making trade-off decisions with incomplete certainty. The data supported the move: low completion rates, clear candidate feedback, a costly dependency we wanted to remove. However, Meritt doesn't charge candidates or employers directly for this feature, so there was no direct revenue metric to point to as justification. The decision required conviction that improving candidate engagement and retiring an expensive dependency would compound into long-term platform value, even without a direct revenue line to attribute it to.

That conviction was validated. The feature became one of the most talked-about parts of the candidate experience, and bringing the capability in-house opened up further improvements that wouldn't have been possible with the previous supplier. The trade-off was worth making, but it required being honest about what the data could confirm and what required judgment.
